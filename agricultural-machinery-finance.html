Agricultural Machinery Finance Explained

Purchasing farm machinery outright ties up significant amounts of capital that could otherwise be used for operational expenses. In agriculture, maintaining liquidity is critical for managing the volatility of seasons and commodity prices. By using finance, a farm can spread the cost of a tractor or harvester over several years. This allows the machine to generate income that helps cover the repayments. It also preserves cash reserves for essential inputs like seed, fertiliser, fuel and labour. This approach ensures that a farm can upgrade ageing equipment or expand its fleet without draining its working capital, which provides a buffer against poor weather or market downturns.

Finance Structures Explained

Selecting the correct finance structure is crucial for maximising tax benefits and managing cash flow. A agricultural machinery arrangement typically falls into three categories. A chattel mortgage involves the business owning the equipment from the start, with the lender holding a mortgage over it. This is often favoured by GST registered businesses for immediate tax credits. A commercial hire purchase sees the lender own the item until the final payment is made, after which ownership transfers to the business. A finance lease is a rental agreement where the lender retains ownership, offering lower monthly payments and flexibility to upgrade at the end of the term. Each option has implications for balance sheet reporting and tax deductions.

Interest Rates and Terms

Cost of finance varies widely based on the risk profile of the borrower. Established operations with a strong trading history generally access the most competitive rates. Indicative data suggests these established farms may see rates between 6.5 and 9 per cent per annum. Operations with a shorter history, perhaps one to two years, typically face rates between 9 and 12 per cent. Newer or specialist operators might encounter rates from 12 to 15 per cent. These figures are indicative and subject to change based on market conditions. Loan terms generally span one to seven years, designed to match the productive life of the asset. This ensures the repayments are proportionate to the value the machinery adds to the farm over time.

Eligibility and Assessment

Lenders evaluate several key factors when deciding whether to approve a loan for farm equipment. The primary consideration is the financial performance and trading history of the farm. Lenders look for consistent income streams to ensure repayments can be met even during off seasons. The type of machinery is also important, as high value assets with strong resale markets like tractors are viewed more favourably than specialised equipment with limited resale potential. The size of the deposit can influence the interest rate, with a larger contribution often reducing the lender's risk. Even newer operators can secure funding, although they should expect higher interest rates and a requirement for a larger deposit compared to established farmers.

Tax and Timing Considerations

Tax efficiency is a major driver of equipment finance decisions. The Australian Taxation Office has specific rules regarding depreciation and GST claims that differ by finance structure. Under a chattel mortgage, businesses can usually claim the GST input tax credit on the purchase price in their next Business Activity Statement. They can also claim depreciation on the asset as it declines in value. In a commercial hire purchase, the GST is claimed progressively over the term of the loan. A finance lease allows the lease payments to be claimed as a tax deduction. Because these rules are complex and subject to change, it is essential to consult with a qualified accountant to determine which structure offers the best after tax outcome for your specific circumstances.

Common questions

What interest rates can I expect? Rates depend on your farm's risk profile. Established farms with strong financials may see rates between 6.5 and 9 per cent. Operations with one to two years of history might see 9 to 12 per cent, while newer operators could face 12 per cent or more.

Can I finance used machinery? Yes, lenders provide finance for both new and quality used equipment. The age and condition of the used machinery will affect the loan term and interest rate, as the asset serves as security for the loan.

How do balloon payments work? A balloon payment is a lump sum due at the end of the loan term. By deferring this portion of the principal to the end, your regular monthly or weekly repayments are reduced. You must plan for this final payment through savings or refinancing.
